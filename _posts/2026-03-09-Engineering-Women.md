*In honor of International Women's Day (March 8th) and Women's History Month, this one's for the girls who looked at how things work and decided to build them better.*

When I was younger, I thought engineers were just dudes in hard hats or guys working on cars. Then I found out my friend's mom was an aerospace engineer, and my whole brain rebooted. Engineering is basically everything—bridges, computers, dishwashers, space shuttles, the electrical grid, your phone. And women have been shaping all of it, even when engineering schools literally wouldn't let them through the door.

Below I'll walk through some of the most influential women in engineering, from the 1800s up to right now. These aren't just history lessons—these are people who looked at problems and said "I can fix that."


## 1) The ones who got it started when nobody wanted them to

Before engineering was even called "engineering," women were out here inventing things.

* Ada Lovelace (1815–1852) – She worked with Charles Babbage on this early computer concept called the Analytical Engine, and she wrote what's basically the first computer program ever. She realized machines could do more than just calculate numbers—they could handle symbols and logic. That's why she's often called the first computer programmer, even though she was working in the 1840s .

* Emily Warren Roebling (1843–1903)– This woman basically built the Brooklyn Bridge. Her husband was the chief engineer but got sick from decompression sickness (the bends) while working on it. Emily stepped in, taught herself about material properties, cable construction, and stress calculations, and managed the whole project. She was the first person to cross the bridge when it opened in 1883 .

* Bertha Lamme (1869–1943) – First female mechanical engineer in the U.S. Graduated from Ohio State in 1893 with a degree in mechanical engineering (specializing in the new field of electrical engineering). Her senior thesis got her hired at Westinghouse, where she worked on major electrical systems. When she walked across that graduation stage, people actually applauded—which was apparently not the norm back then .

## 2) The early 1900s crew: breaking doors down

* Lillian Moller Gilbreth (1878–1972) – Industrial engineer and psychologist with TWELVE kids. You might know her from the book *Cheaper by the Dozen*, which was about her family. But professionally, she basically invented modern kitchen design—things like foot-pedal trash cans and shelves inside refrigerator doors came from her. She wanted to make household work more efficient so women could do other things with their lives. First woman elected to the National Academy of Engineering .

* Kate Gleason (1865–1933) – First woman to study mechanical engineering at Cornell. She took over her dad's machine shop when money got tight and helped build it into Gleason Corporation, which still makes gear-cutting machines today. She also designed affordable housing for workers and became the first woman president of a national bank. Pretty solid resume .

* Nora Stanton Blatch Barney (1883–1971) – First woman to get a civil engineering degree from Cornell (1905). Granddaughter of Elizabeth Cady Stanton, so activism ran in the family. She worked as an engineer and architect, but when she tried to get promoted in the American Society of Civil Engineers, they denied her. She kept going anyway .

## 3) Mid-century: war, space, and getting hired

World War II changed everything. Men went to fight, and suddenly companies needed engineers, badly. Programs like the **Curtiss-Wright Engineering Program** trained hundreds of women in aircraft design and production. They were called "Engineering Cadettes" .

* Edith Clarke (1883–1959) – First female electrical engineer in the U.S. She invented the Clarke calculator, which was basically an early graphing calculator that solved equations for power transmission lines. Before that, engineers did all that math by hand. She was also the first woman to present a paper to the American Institute of Electrical Engineers .

* Mary Jackson (1921–2005) – NASA's first Black female engineer. Started as a "human computer" (yes, that was a job title), then fought for permission to take graduate-level courses so she could become an engineer. Spent decades at NASA, co-authored like a dozen research papers, and her story was in *Hidden Figures* .

* Willie Hobbs Moore (1934–1994) – First African American woman to earn a PhD in physics in the U.S., but before that, she got bachelor's and master's degrees in electrical engineering from University of Michigan. Worked at Bendix, Barnes Engineering, and eventually Ford, where she helped bring Japanese manufacturing methods to the company .

## 4) Space age and beyond

* Yvonne Brill (1924–2013) – Rocket scientist. Literally. She invented a better propulsion system for satellites (the hydrazine resistojet) that kept them in orbit longer. For decades, she might've been the only woman in the U.S. doing rocket science. Got the National Medal of Technology from President Obama .

* Sally Ride (1951–2012) – First American woman in space (1983). But after NASA, she became a physics professor and started Sally Ride Science to get girls interested in STEM. Wrote science books for kids and spoke at hundreds of events for young women. Understood that representation actually matters .

* Aprille Ericsson (b. 1963) – Aerospace engineer at NASA. First African American woman to get a PhD in mechanical engineering from Howard, first African American woman to earn an engineering PhD at NASA Goddard. Works on satellite missions and instrument development. Also coaches youth sports and speaks to kids everywhere about STEM. Grew up in Brooklyn projects, got bussed to school, and just kept going .

## 5) Living legends and current geniuses

* Kimberly Bryant (b. 1967) – Electrical engineer who worked at Pfizer, Merck, and other biotech companies. But she's most known for founding **Black Girls Code** in 2011, because her daughter wanted to learn programming and there were no good options. The organization has now taught thousands of girls of color to code .

* Jessica Matthews (b. 1988) – Invented the SOCCKET—a soccer ball that generates energy while you play. Half hour of play powers an LED light for three hours. She saw diesel generators running at her aunt's wedding in Nigeria and wanted to create cleaner energy solutions. Founded Uncharted Power and keeps innovating .

* Katya Echazarreta (b. 1996) – Electrical engineer and first Mexican-born woman to go to space (2022). Worked at NASA on the Europa Clipper mission (that's a Jupiter moon mission). Also hosts science TV shows and tells everyone who'll listen that space is for all of us .

* Christina Cyr – Electrical engineer who designs circuit boards for phones and IoT devices. Holds multiple patents. Also designed her own phone—the Cyrcle Phone—which has a circular design and is open-source. Has taught hundreds of people how to build their own smartphones .

## 6) Why this still matters right now

Here's the thing: engineering is still not equal. According to EngineeringUK, only about **15.7%** of engineering and technology roles are held by women in the UK . In the U.S., women make up around **20%** of engineering graduates, but the numbers are lower for mechanical, electrical, and computer engineering . For women of color, the percentages drop even more.

That's not because women can't do engineering. It's because the system wasn't built for them—and sometimes still isn't. The women in this list faced professors who told them to save their father's money, companies that made them retire when they got married, and professional societies that refused to promote them.

They kept going anyway.

And because they did, we have better satellites, safer bridges, smarter kitchens, and girls who code.
